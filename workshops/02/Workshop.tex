\section{Workshop 02}

In this workshop, you will derive the famous formula relating the dot
product of two vectors with the cosine of the angle between them. The
exercise makes fundamental use of the relationship
\[ \vec{v} \cdot \vec{v} = \lVert \vec{v} \rVert^2 \].

\subsection{Warm-up}

\begin{questions}

\question Pick two random points with integer coordinates between $-10$
and $10$. Plot these points on a separate piece of paper, and draw
vectors from the origin to your two points. These are the vectors
$\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$. Draw the vector $\vec{v} - \vec{w}$ as the
third side of the evident triangle, paying attention to its direction.

\dwrspace{1}

\question Let $\vec{v} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$. Verify that
$\vec{v} \cdot \vec{v} = \lVert \vec{v} \rVert^2$. (Don't make this
harder than it is.)

\dwrspace{1}

\question Evaluate $(\vec{v} - \vec{w}) \cdot (\vec{v} - \vec{w})$ in
two different ways: in one way, using the fundamental relation given
above; in another, using the algebraic laws given in Theorem 1 of
section 12.3. Carefully justify your calculations in terms of these
laws. You obtain an equation (equate the two different formulas you
derived). Write it down.

\dwrspace{1}

\question What does the Law of Cosines have to say about your triangle?
Formulate your answer in terms of the vectors that are the sides. (The
angle you want to focus on is the one formed by $\vec{v}$ and
\$\vec{w}.)

\dwrspace{1}

\question Compare the results of the last two steps, and derive the
famous dot product--cosine relation,
\[ \vec{v} \cdot \vec{w} = \lVert v \rVert \lVert w \rVert \cos{\theta},\]
where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$.

\textbackslash{}end\{questions\}
